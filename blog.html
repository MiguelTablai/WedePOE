DIY Cold Brew Guide

For a perfectly smooth and delicious cold brew coffee made at home, follow these steps: STEP 1: Choose your favorite Cold Brew Grinds, or grind your own beans coarsely. Mix ground coffee with room temperature water in a mason jar, and steep for 16-18 hours. We recommend starting with a ratio of 1 cup of grinds to 3 cups of water. STEP 2: Strain your cold brew through a cheesecloth or a coffee filter-lined mesh strainer. STEP 3: Pour over ice and enjoy! Add a splash of water or cream if needed.

The Best Coffee Beans For Cold Brew

Cold brew coffee is surprisingly popular in South Africa. Although not always practical in an office setting where “time is money”, a cold brew can be an enjoyable experience at home, especially when you’re after a bit of self care and relaxation. The key to a good cold brew is making sure that the grind is done correctly. A coarse grind will always be a winner when it comes to this type of coffee. Fine grounds are more likely to offer a bitter taste, and over extraction won’t yield the best results for a cold brew. Before that happens, it’s important to have the right coffee beans in hand for the job. Cold brew coffee is made through the process of steeping coffee in water for a number of hours at room temperature (or something slightly colder). This offers a slower, low temperature brew that changes the taste relative to what you might expect from the usual hot brew. The same coffee beans will end up tasting wildly different when you make a cold brew, and often low acidity fruit flavours are accentuated through this process. Try a hot and cold brew for your favourite coffee beans to note the differences between the two, and to see which one you prefer. A common question about cold brew coffee is whether it is stronger than a “regular” (hot brew) coffee. Because more caffeine is extracted during a hot brew, it typically ends up being stronger than its counterpart. There is an exception to the rule, however. Iced coffee typically has a lower caffeine content than cold brew, which is again down to how it is made in the first place. If you’re trying to manage your daily caffeine intake a little bit better, a cold brew is a good way to gradually work your way down the scale gently instead of going cold turkey and feeling the negative effects immediately. Medium or dark roast are the best coffee beans for cold brew, most notably because of the ability of the process to extract maximum flavour out of them. If neither of these are to your taste, try other beans that you’re more familiar with instead. As with any brewing process, the key to getting the best coffee cup at the end of the day is to pay attention to the size of the grind. If you’re used to making your coffee in a specific way, try the same grind coarseness and note the differences in taste. Experimenting is a key part of igniting the inner barista in you, so open your mind to different tastes and flavours for the best possible experience. Don’t set the bar too high for your first cold brew. It will take a few tries before you find the right mix and balance for your palette. The team at Aquaspresso are here to help if you have any questions about the process. Once refined, expect a light, crisp and refreshing tasting cup of coffee. It will preserve much of its acidity, especially if you achieve maximum extraction. A bit of milk might do the trick as well, so play with your options and remember to have fun along the way too.

Revolutionizing Cold Brew: The Science Behind Ultrasonic Coffee

Cold brew—once a niche indulgence—is now a staple in coffee culture, beloved for its smoothness and caffeine punch. But its lengthy brewing process has always been a hurdle, until now. Imagine a cold brew ready in just three minutes, thanks to the power of ultrasonic sound waves. Here’s how scientists are shaking up the coffee world with this groundbreaking method. Cold brew enthusiasts, rejoice! The days of waiting 24 to 48 hours for your beloved elixir are over. Researchers at the University of New South Wales, Sydney, Australia have unveiled a game-changing technique using ultrasonic waves to accelerate the cold brew process to mere minutes. What started as an exploration into antioxidant extraction turned into a coffee revelation. Initially, the team experimented with high-end ultrasonic equipment, which comes with a hefty price tag. However, their breakthrough came when they adapted the method to utilize standard espresso machines—a more accessible option for cafes and coffee aficionados alike. By passing cold water through an untamped espresso puck and subjecting it to controlled bursts of ultrasonic waves, they achieved remarkable results. In their study, soon to be published in Ultrasonic Sonochemistry, researchers detailed their findings on how ultrasonic waves enhance coffee extraction. This process, known as “acoustic cavitation,” involves the rapid formation and collapse of microscopic bubbles within the coffee grounds. These bubbles effectively “smash up” the grounds, accelerating the extraction of flavours, oils, and aromatic compounds. During their experiments, varying the duration of ultrasonic exposure revealed intriguing nuances. A one-minute exposure produced a cold brew comparable to a traditional 24-hour brew, albeit with reduced aroma intensity. Conversely, a three-minute exposure amplified the aroma but introduced a touch of bitterness, signalling slight over-extraction. Fine-tuning this method, the researchers settled on an optimal balance, achieving robust flavour extraction akin to a lengthy cold brew, but in a fraction of the time. What sets ultrasonic cold brew apart? According to Francisco Trujillo, lead author of the study, the results speak for themselves. The brew boasts a satisfying mouthfeel, enhanced viscosity, and a pleasantly balanced acidity—a profile that resonates well with coffee enthusiasts seeking a rich, yet smooth coffee experience. Unlike traditional cold brews, which can sometimes exhibit bitterness due to prolonged steeping, ultrasonic cold brews offer a cleaner, more vibrant flavour profile. Moreover, the visual appeal of ultrasonic cold brew is striking. Its opaque appearance, a result of emulsified oils from the coffee grounds, adds a distinct visual allure that contrasts with the clarity of traditional cold brews. This visual and sensory enhancement further underscores its appeal among coffee connoisseurs. For coffee shops and home brewers alike, adopting ultrasonic technology could revolutionize operations. The ability to produce cold brew on demand opens up new possibilities for menu offerings and customer satisfaction. Imagine satisfying the demand for cold brew during peak hours without the usual wait times—increasing efficiency while delighting patrons. Looking ahead, further research into refining the ultrasonic process could lead to even more nuanced flavours and textures. As technology evolves and becomes more accessible, expect to see innovations that democratise high-quality coffee experiences beyond traditional methods.
